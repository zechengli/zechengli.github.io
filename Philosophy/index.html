Philosophy

1. 有神论与世界结构

I am a theist. I believe there exists an intelligence infinitely beyond human capability that created this world through an ingenious process. To itself, this process is concrete; to us, it is abstract and inexplicable. We are to this existence what cells are to our own bodies—tiny and simple.

This world is likely an infinite computer simulation, but its logic must be more complex than binary 0 and 1. There must be states between 0 and 1—quantum states. To explain this, I think of π. There are three possibilities for π:

1. Terminating decimal: Meaning we could draw a truly perfect circle, but current human computing power and physical space cannot construct it. A compass-drawn circle is only the most ideal approximation. Philosophically, everything is predetermined. The perfect diameter and perfect circumference would have an endpoint.

2. Infinite repeating decimal: Meaning history cycles infinitely—seasons repeat, and humanity oscillates between desire and boredom like Sisyphus pushing his boulder uphill every day.

3. Infinite non-repeating decimal: Meaning history is an open, complex world—unpredictable.

I believe all three are possible, or perhaps the third encompasses the first two.

How the creator communicates with us: through lightning creating organic matter, through random events that seem "natural." But nothing is truly accidental—everything is predetermined. Emergence itself was introduced to humanity through lightning, or perhaps through extraterrestrials.

2. 《黑客帝国》的先知对话

There's a classic dialogue between Neo and the Oracle: "You're not here to make the choice. You've already made it (your future self). You're here to understand why you made it." I've listened to that line 100-200 times and let my emotions unfold inside it. The takeaway for me: we don't have free will. What we do now is already decided. At every moment we carry all prior physical states plus a touch of randomness, and that pushes our life forward. Looking back from the future, everything seems explainable, but in the moment we don't understand—only after it happens do we understand.

1) Simplicity, sincerity, and kindness. As long as you are 95% honest and consistently maintain it, people will trust you. On the other hand, I like to show respect and honesty toward those above me, but without flattery. Show compassion for peers and those facing difficult circumstances. Overall, don't trouble others unnecessarily—handle things to the best of your ability on your own, but when you can't, seek help boldly and courageously.

2) On another note, don't interfere with others' karma, but don't completely reject random connections either.

3) Toward those who appreciate and have helped you, give your full feedback. Toward those with negative energy, try to avoid them.

I believe everyone needs an underlying logic in life, a main quest. This quest should ideally make the world a better place, while also allowing us to help those around us live better lives. For me, following my mentors is the simplest way; improving upon what others have built is another. But with AI becoming so powerful, I often feel lost, unsure of what truly aligns with this underlying logic. We are all just small boats drifting with the current, so perhaps the most important thing is to make our own boat sturdy.

My View on Research

I think the most important thing in doing research is honesty—you must be honest with your data, honest with your experimental results. Don't deceive yourself. Even if the results are disappointing, or if your hypothesis is proven wrong, you must accept it. The worst thing is to force a narrative that doesn't exist, just to get a paper published. In the end, who are you deceiving? Only yourself.

A person shouldn't do research just to publish a paper, but should choose something meaningful to do.

A meaningful direction doesn't necessarily have to be something groundbreaking and unprecedented. It can be simply improving on something that already exists and making it better.

If we were to go back to ancient times and help people build houses, the houses would probably be much better. In this way, we have also done something meaningful.

The process of doing research is like climbing a mountain—what matters is not the result, but the effort along the way. Often when you reach the summit, you realize it's nothing special, but the sweat and scenery during the journey are what's truly precious. Of course, sometimes you work hard and still don't get results, but the process itself has already made you a better person.

Nowadays, AI is becoming more and more powerful, and many papers are generated by AI. Many people say that research will soon be taken over by AI, so why bother doing research at all? I think this is a question that doesn't need to be considered right now. Perhaps it's more appropriate to think about it after we have truly learned to use AI.

AI is just like a mobile phone or a computer—it's merely a tool. We don't need to fear it; instead, we should learn how to use it properly. AI can't take over what humans do because it's all about human nature.

My View on Work

For work, the most important thing is to get things done. Everything else is secondary.

But of course, if you can find something you enjoy doing and are good at, that's the best outcome.

For me, this work is research. I happen to enjoy it and I'm also somewhat good at it, so I will keep doing it.

The important thing is to be happy while doing it and to enjoy the process—that's enough.

For academia, I think the most important thing is honesty—you must be honest with your data, honest with your experimental results. Don't deceive yourself. Even if the results are disappointing, or if your hypothesis is proven wrong, you must accept it. The worst thing is to force a narrative that doesn't exist, just to get a paper published. In the end, who are you deceiving? Only yourself.

However, what I'm doing now is not good enough yet, and I will continue to work hard.
